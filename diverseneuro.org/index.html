Inclusivity in Neuroscience

Does interdisciplinarity translate into intersectionality? Let’s look at what the data has to say.

Survey starts soon!*

Why this Project?

As scientists, we are trained to overlook the personal and subjective and optimise the achievable objectivity in our work. Our fundamental dogma, so to speak, preaches unbiased analysis and aims to remove the observer from the observed. In giving ourselves completely over to this practice, we sometimes cannot come to terms with the harsh reality of the non-theoretical: We are people.

Why Neuroscience?

Propelled by the tenets of machine learning and artificial intelligence[1,2], neuroscience is easily one of the fastest-growing disciplines in STEM [3,4,5]. Few disciplines enjoy the truly interdisciplinary status that neuroscience does. A brilliant confluence of a large number of disciplines, ranging from statistical mechanics to theory of mind to cognition and biology to information theory, Neuroscience thrives on its interdisciplinarity [6].

Several parent disciplines of the field, particularly those under Physics and Mathematics, have long been stereotypically perceived as 'male domains' [7,8], while most women in science are found in disciplines such as psychology and life sciences, commonly perceived as the 'softer sciences'. Studies have shown that the former disciplines prove to be more hostile to minority groups such as LGBTQIA+ [9,10] , whereas fields with a larger representation of women are safer spaces [11,12,13]. While in recent times there have been more inquiries into the representation of women in STEM, data about minority demographics such as LGBTQIA+ in STEM have been as sparse as the representation itself [14].

How will studying this help?

With our study, we aim to understand the extent of inclusivity in an emergent intersectional community such as neuroscience (including the AI and cognitive psychology communities), a community that we ourselves belong to. We believe that the Neurosciences community is fertile ground not only to sample from, but to also propel early proactive course-correcting if necessary. As people of Science, it is essential that we understand any biases or perceived threats to inclusivity, so that we can move to combat inequality and discrimination. Let data lead the way.

Ethics Approval

We are commited to a transparent and ethical process. The survey is currently in the process of being reviewed by IISER Ethics Committee for Human Research (IECHR) at IISER Pune and the Institutional Review Board at Ashoka University. The survey will ensue upon approval from the aforementioned committees. We are devoted to maintaining integrity in our work, and while delays are frustrating, we want to ensure in every possible way that our project is tested against the highest standards of safety.

Ensuring anonymity

The survey is designed to be completely anonymous using Google Forms, with email address collection disabled. Further, we do not collect any information that can be used to single out the identity of any individual respondent. All demographics are collected at a geographical region level to assure higher levels of anonymity.

Survey: Diversity trends

We aim to collect information about diversity in race, gender identity and sexual orientations in the neuroscience community across different geographical regions around the globe in order to assess the representation of different minority communities. We hypothesize that due to its high interdisciplinarity, the neuroscience community will have a more balanced representation across diverse identities.

Survey: Inclusivity trends

By collecting information from respondents about the general outlook towards inclusivity of minorities such as LGBTQIA+ individuals in their local academic climate and their personal experiences with perceived discrimination/bias, we aim to discover any significant biases across different regions and perceived threats to different minorities.

Publicity for Impact

We plan to collaborate with various online neuroscience conferences that have been organised in the COVID-19 world and LGBT/Queer in STEM organisations in order to publicise the survey. We also plan to use social media to spread the survey, particularly the highly active NeuroTwitter community. At the end of the study, the results will be released publicly and reported to all interested individuals.

Data availability

At the end of the study, the collected anonymous survey data will be made public to allow individuals to run their own analysis. All code for our analysis will also be made public to promote transparency and reproducibility.
